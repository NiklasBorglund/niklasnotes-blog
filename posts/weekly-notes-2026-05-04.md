Annapurna Interactive published a new Walking Simulator named Mixtape. Alabaster Dawn launches to Overwhelmingly Positive reviews. 
Dead as Disco brings stylish rhythm combat with a custom music twist. And Shiro Games enters the open world RPG space with Farever, though a rocky technical launch is holding it back for now.

Let's have a look!

---

## Mixtape by Beethoven and Dinosaur

```condensedgamecard
{
"gameId": "120435"
}
```

A new walking simulator published by Annapurna Interactive, where three friends relive their last night together. Skateboarding, fireworks, teenage antics, all set to a classic 90s soundtrack.

Players frequently call out the music selection as a standout positive. Given the name of the game, I guess that's a must have.  The visuals and art style also get appreciation, though a few find them inconsistent.

Main criticism is where the game feels like an interactive movie more than a game. A lot of players like more actual gameplay in these types of games than purely story choices. 
Some reviewers also call the story clichéd and the characters unlikable or stereotypical.
The 90s nostalgia angle also divides people, with some feeling it¨s good and others not so much.

I guess this is the kind of game where expectations going in matter a lot. If you want a short emotional story with a great soundtrack, it seems to deliver. If you want to have something more mechanics heavy, probably not for you.

## Dead as Disco by Brain Jar Games, Inc.

```condensedgamecard
{
"gameId": "201578"
}
```

Rhythm-based action game in Early Access where you play a fallen music icon seeking revenge against his ex-bandmates. 
Stylish combat syncs to the soundtrack in a colorful cyberpunk world.

Overwhelmingly Positive, and the custom music integration is mentioned a lot. Players can import their own music, which adds a whole layer to it. '
The art direction and vibrant visual style get consistent love, and the combat mechanics with rhythm elements works well.

The main criticism is content volume for the price with players wanting more. Boss battle design gets called out for pacing and difficulty issues, and the story feels incomplete.

## Farever by Shiro Games

```condensedgamecard
{
"gameId": "209896"
}
```

[Northgard](https://niklasnotes.com/dashboard/game/2448/northgard_definitive_edition) is one of my favorite strategy games, so whenever Shiro Games are releasing something new I'm immediately intrigued.

Farever is an open world action-adventure RPG in released Early Access. You explore dungeons and tackle challenges solo or with others. A lot of players seem to compare this game to Zelda in terms of exploration.

It launched to Mixed reviews, and the main culprit seems to be server instabilities with players frequently experiencing lag, disconnections and crashes. Performance optimization adds to it with poor frame rates and stuttering

Although when I check the reviews manually, a lot of the reviews are genuinely excited about the game and what it can evolve into during EA. Combat mechanics gets praised, character progression feels rewarding, and the exploration resonates with players.

Will be interesting how it will turn out after they've fixed the technical issues.

## Everything is Crab: The Animal Evolution Roguelite by Odd Dreams Digital

```condensedgamecard
{
"gameId": "180276"
}
```

A roguelite from developers based in Malmö here in Sweden. They [released a demo](https://niklasnotes.com/dashboard/game/258799/everything_is_crab_the_animal_evolution_roguelite_demo) back in february with 98(!) percent positive reviews.

The full game launched with 80% positive though. Interestingly, it's mainly Simplified Chinese reviews that's lowering the score with other audiences giving it around 90%+ or over. 
The demo had a strong Chinese Presence too, but there it was almost exclusively positive. Interesting divide, but maybe it is just being judged harder when it's a paid product.

The creature evolution mechanics are what players mention the most, with diverse build options and unique playstyles. The charming pixel art style gets appreciation too, and replayability holds up well due to the varied builds.
Difficulty balance in later stages is the strongest complaint, and that it starts feeling repetitive after a while.

## Alabaster Dawn by Radical Fish Games

```condensedgamecard
{
"gameId": "31996"
}
```

The pixel art in this game is insanely good, remember that I saw footage of this when they released their very [popular demo](https://niklasnotes.com/dashboard/game/230134/alabaster_dawn_demo) the last Next fest. Back in december, they reported that they were closing in [on 250k wishlists](https://www.radicalfishgames.com/?p=7879). I can only imagine that that number has increased quite a lot since.

It's a JRPG set in a ruined world. Radical Fish Games previously made [CrossCode](https://niklasnotes.com/dashboard/game/135403/crosscode), another very popular JRPG. It seems like they know this genre well.

Alabaster Dawn launched with Overwhelmingly Positive. Players love how fluid the combat feels. And as I mentioned above, players also notice the gorgeous pixel art an animations. The story engagement and character depth are mentioned as positives too

The main criticism is the length of the game during early access, some players even recommend others to wait until the full game is released.
Technical performance on Steam Deck gets flagged, and localization quality is an issue for non-English speakers. Overall, not much criticism it seems.

## Early Access Graduates

The main complaints are bugs and lack of polish, clumsy controls particularly around building and vehicles, and graphics that feel lacking compared to similar titles.
